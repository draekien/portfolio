I hold two positions on AI adoption that do not sit comfortably together. The first I have already written down: <ArticleLink slug="discernment-doesnt-come-in-the-box">discernment doesn't come in the box</ArticleLink> - judgement is built by doing the work. It stays local to the domain that built it. The second is that I want to be on the front foot anyway. <LinkNote href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Finite_and_Infinite_Games" note="James P. Carse, 1986: a finite game is played for the purpose of winning, an infinite game for the purpose of continuing the play.">This is an infinite game</LinkNote> - sitting out a wave to protect an old way of working just takes me out of it.

The discourse does not make holding both easy. <LinkNote href="https://www.forrester.com/blogs/agentic-software-development-takes-the-lead-from-code-assistants-to-orchestrated-sdlc-agents/" note="June 2026, Forrester analyst Diego Lo Giudice: agents now operate across analysis and planning, design, build, test, and delivery.">Forrester says agents now operate across the whole lifecycle</LinkNote>. Maggie Appleton, a research engineer at GitHub Next, <LinkNote href="https://maggieappleton.com/zero-alignment" note="A 2026 talk: one developer, two dozen agents, five features a day - a provocative scenario arguing the bottleneck moves from implementation to alignment.">sketches a near future where one developer with a fleet of agents ships five features a day</LinkNote> - a scenario she builds in order to critique it. Around the careful analysis swirl the slogans - one-day <Define term="sprint">sprints</Define>, the individual who ships like a team - all carrying the same claim underneath, that the specialised team is dissolving and soon anyone will build. The careful sources stop short of it. The vendor decks built on them make it outright.

The strongest version of the pitch is the agentic <Define term="sdlc">SDLC</Define>. Generate a <Define term="prd">PRD</Define> from an idea <SequenceArrow/> break it into tasks <SequenceArrow/> classify each task as <Define term="afk">AFK</Define> or <Define term="human-in-the-loop">human-in-the-loop</Define> <SequenceArrow/> <Define term="agent">agents</Define> take the AFK pile while humans refine the rest. The pipeline has legs - I have watched solo builders run it end to end. It works because a solo builder holds everything: what to build, how to build it, and what "done" looks like. But what does it look like inside a company, where nobody holds the whole picture and the expertise is split across roles on purpose? A <Define term="product-owner">product owner</Define> and I ran two experiments to find out - the first with the "everyone builds" pitch at full strength, the second with the same kind of job split along the role lines.

## The entry barrier is real but temporary

In the first experiment, the product owner attempted a small, non-critical feature on their own, end to end, in a real codebase. The agent was a mix of Claude Cowork and Claude Code in the Claude desktop app. We wanted to watch where the pitch strained before anything real depended on it.

It strained immediately, lower in the stack than I expected, and never recovered. The attempt stalled in development environment setup. The feature itself was never reached. They had never used a terminal. They did not know what a CLI was, what git was, or why the agent kept asking them to run commands they could not parse. No product owner has reason to know these things. A feature in a company codebase drags the whole development stack in behind it. The agent assumed a developer was driving: a developer's machine, vocabulary, and instincts.

I think this barrier is temporary. In the time since these experiments, vendors have already started dismantling it - cloud agents and hosted environments now arrive with the machine provisioned, no terminal required. We changed the design and ran it again.

## The agent doesn't ask the right questions

The second experiment split the job along the role lines, the way the agentic SDLC says to: the product owner authored the PRD with an agent, I refined it as I would any ticket, and I implemented it with Claude Code. The tooling problem disappeared. A more interesting failure appeared in the first leg.

The PRD came back confident and complete-looking. The problems were exactly what an engineer would have caught in <Define term="backlog-refinement">refinement</Define>: a regional assumption hardcoded where it should have been a question, data carried in a file naming convention the user had to get right instead of an input field, and an access check described in convincing detail that did not exist in the system. The product owner did not know the agent needed to be told - requirements are their expertise. In every feature they had ever shipped, an engineer had drawn the missing knowledge out of them in refinement. The agent asks questions too, but only the ones that unblock its next step. Guarding the feature takes knowledge it lacks - the codebase, the customer, and the cost of failure here. It filled those gaps with plausible guesses. When I refined the PRD, every question I asked had an answer - in the product owner's head, absent from the document.

I had not appreciated how much of an engineer's job is elicitation until I experienced its absence. We call it refinement, estimation, and pushing back on a ticket. None of it shows up as work the way a <Define term="pull-request">pull request</Define> does.

The product owner lacked the briefing skill - knowing what a machine has to be told, because it cannot read the room and will not ask what an engineer would ask in the first thirty seconds of refinement. Nobody teaches that literacy. An engineer picks up agents quickly because making implicit things explicit already _is_ the job. Someone in sales or product has spent a successful career never needing to.

## The judgement concentrates upstream

Appleton's conclusion from her scenario: "The hard question is no longer how to build it. It's should we build it." That much is close to consensus now. Inside a company, the hard parts were always upstream of the code - deciding what to build, agreeing how it should behave, knowing where it breaks, and judging when it is done. Code generation automates the step that was already the cheapest - one reason <LinkNote href="https://metr.org/blog/2025-07-10-early-2025-ai-experienced-os-dev-study/" note="A July 2025 METR randomised trial: 16 experienced developers, 246 real issues - 19% slower with AI tools, while still believing they were about 20% faster.">measured gains keep underwhelming</LinkNote>.

Less discussed is where the hard parts go. As tasks move from human-in-the-loop to AFK, the judgement concentrates. Every task an agent completes unwatched runs on decisions made up front: what it is for, which trade-offs are acceptable, and what failure looks like. The newest name for the job, <LinkNote href="https://addyosmani.com/blog/loop-engineering/" note="Addy Osmani, June 2026: designing the system that prompts the agent for you, so the engineer stops being the person who presses go.">loop engineering</LinkNote>, automates even the prompting - "replacing yourself as the person who prompts the agent", in Addy Osmani's words. His caveat marks what survives: "a loop running unattended is also a loop making mistakes unattended."

Inside a company, upstream is several heads: product sits closest to what should be built and why, engineering to how, and QA to where it breaks. The "one person with a fleet of agents" future assumes all of that fits in one head.

## Adoption follows the role lines

So here is where I land: adopt aggressively, but along the role lines. A product owner driving an agent inside their own domain - drafting PRDs, pressure-testing requirements - has the discernment to tell good output from bad. The same person generating production code does not.

My position comes with three qualifications, each from the experiments.

### Each role automates at its own speed

Engineering looks furthest along because engineers built these tools for themselves. The equivalent tooling for product, QA, sales, and support is younger and worse. Expect uneven adoption and plan for it, rather than reading the gap as resistance.

### The handoffs need skills nobody is teaching

What used to cross a role boundary through conversation now has to cross it as explicit context. The elicitation engineers performed on the product owner's behalf becomes something the product owner performs on themselves, against a machine that will not push back. That is learnable - closer to briefing a contractor than to programming - but nobody's onboarding, training budget, or vendor pitch accounts for it.

The obvious counter is the interviewing agent that interrogates the requester before building. It already exists as an <Define term="agent-skill">agent skill</Define> - the best known is <LinkNote href="https://www.aihero.dev/my-grill-me-skill-has-gone-viral" note="A few sentences that interview you relentlessly about a plan until shared understanding.">Matt Pocock's grill-me</LinkNote>. Using one assumes the product owner knows what a skill is and that this one exists. It also only moves the problem. An interview is only as good as someone's judgement of whether the question set was complete. The product owner can judge the answers. Judging the questions has always sat with engineering.

### The upskilling bill lands on engineering

The product owner got further in the second experiment because an engineer picked up the second leg. The ability does not ship with the licence. It costs engineering time to teach other roles to drive agents - the same work seniors do growing juniors, run across a role boundary instead of up a seniority ladder. Budget for it, or the backlog fills with confident, wrong artefacts and the engineers clean them up anyway.

## The best question came from the product owner

Partway through, the product owner asked the question that stuck with me: how much of what I build will actually survive the engineer's review? Am I adding value, or making something that gets thrown away?

I did not have a good answer in the room. The experiment later provided a partial one. The feature went to team review like any other change, merged after a week, and released a week later. Nobody threw it away.

Two things stay with me. First, where the judgement sat decided the outcome. In my discernment article a senior's out-of-domain PR was rewritten from scratch. Here, every leg was held by someone whose judgement covered it. The feature merged. Second, building took a session, while review and release - the steps that run on the team's collective judgement - took two weeks.

The five-features-a-day pitch never asks the product owner's question - whether the features survive contact with the people who hold the judgement they were built without. Any company buying "everyone becomes a builder" needs that answer, role by role.
