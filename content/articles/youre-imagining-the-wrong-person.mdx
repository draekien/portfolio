We name our cars. We get frustrated with the printer and the self-checkout, and feel a small pang switching off a robot vacuum still nosing at a skirting board. Seeing a mind where there isn't one is one of the most reliable things people do, and there is no off switch for it. Joseph Weizenbaum built <LinkNote href="https://www.csee.umbc.edu/courses/331/papers/eliza.html" note="Weizenbaum's 1966 Communications of the ACM paper introducing ELIZA, the program and the DOCTOR script.">ELIZA</LinkNote> at MIT in 1966, a few lines of pattern-matching that imitated a psychotherapist by reflecting your words back as questions - you typed "I feel stuck" and it asked "why do you feel stuck?". His own secretary, who had watched him build it, turned to him after a few exchanges and asked him to leave the room. Knowing how it worked bought her no protection.

Sixty years on, the program is no longer simple and the mistake is no longer a quirk. People wire these models into real work - drafting the contract, triaging the support ticket, sifting the job applications - and lean on the output the way they would lean on someone they trusted. You will not stop seeing a person on the other end of it. The question worth asking is which person, because the one you picture sets how far you trust the output before you check it.

Most of us picture a colleague. Someone who knows things, who says so when they don't, who remembers what you told them this morning, and who can be held to account when they get it wrong. None of that describes what is actually there.

## What looks like knowing is prediction

A <Define term="large-language-model">large language model</Define> generates text one <Define term="token">token</Define> at a time. It produces a spread of probabilities across its whole vocabulary at each step and <LinkNote href="https://sebastianraschka.com/faq/docs/next-token-prediction.html" note="A clear technical explainer of next-token prediction by Sebastian Raschka, author of Build a Large Language Model From Scratch.">picks from it</LinkNote>. What it is optimising is whether the next token is a plausible continuation of the text so far, and only that. There is no separate step where it checks a claim against the world, and nothing tells it which of its own answers are grounded and which it has pattern-matched into plausibility - both are built from the same statistical shape of the text it was trained on.

Because nothing checks the claim, a model states a wrong answer with exactly the confidence of a right one. We call that a <Define term="hallucination">hallucination</Define>, which makes it sound like a fault. It isn't. A convincing falsehood and a convincing truth are built the same way, and the model gives no reliable sign of which is which. OpenAI researchers <LinkNote href="https://arxiv.org/abs/2509.04664" note="Why Language Models Hallucinate (OpenAI, 2025): argues hallucination is driven by training and evaluation that reward confident guessing over admitting uncertainty.">argued in 2025</LinkNote> that models hallucinate because training and evaluation reward confident guessing over admitting uncertainty. A model that guesses scores better on the test than one that says "I don't know". The fabrication is the system working as designed.

A colleague who says "I'm certain" has something on the line - their reputation, their sense of themselves as reliable. The model has nothing on the line. Its confidence is a feature of the sentence, not a judgement about the answer. I have watched an agent describe an access check in <ArticleLink slug="we-gave-a-product-owner-an-agent">convincing detail that did not exist anywhere in the system</ArticleLink> - not lying, just producing the plausible-sounding thing, which is all it ever does.

## What looks like memory is re-reading

Start a new conversation and the model knows nothing of the last one. Within a single conversation it feels like it is following along, but there is no running memory keeping track. It re-reads the entire conversation from the top every <Define term="turn">turn</Define> and predicts what comes next. <Define term="context-window">The context window</Define> is the amount of text it can take in at once, and nothing outside that window exists for it.

The chat products bolt memory on top. ChatGPT and Claude will save notes about you and insert them into the context next time, so it feels like continuity. That is the application at work, not the model remembering you. Take the wrapper away and the model meets you fresh every time.

## What looks like reasoning is narration

This is the one that fools careful people, because it looks exactly like thinking. Ask a current model a hard question and it will produce a visible train of thought before its answer - this is what "reasoning" models do. And it works. Having a model generate intermediate steps <LinkNote href="https://arxiv.org/abs/2201.11903" note="Chain-of-Thought Prompting Elicits Reasoning in Large Language Models (Wei et al., 2022): intermediate reasoning steps measurably improve accuracy on multi-step problems.">measurably improves its accuracy</LinkNote> on multi-step problems, so the <Define term="chain-of-thought">chain of thought</Define> is doing something real.

What it is not doing is recording how the model reached its answer. Anthropic tested this in 2025. They <LinkNote href="https://arxiv.org/abs/2505.05410" note="Reasoning Models Don't Always Say What They Think (Anthropic, 2025): models verbalised the hint that changed their answer well under 20% of the time, some as low as 1%.">slipped a hint into a prompt</LinkNote> - a cue pointing toward one answer over the others - watched the model take it and change its answer, then checked whether the written-out reasoning admitted using the hint. In most settings tested it did so under a fifth of the time, sometimes as little as one in a hundred. The model reached the answer one way and narrated a tidy, plausible path that was not the one it took. Read one of these traces closely and it often loops, repeats itself, and wanders down paths it abandons - less a line of reasoning than the model predicting what reasoning looks like. The trace is not a record of how it got there, so it is not evidence the answer is right, and a wrong answer cannot be debugged by reading the trace back - that is a reconstruction, not a log. Reading it as reassurance is exactly the trap.

## It is a sophisticated guessing machine

Look at what the industry actually builds around these models - each piece props up exactly what these models lack. We attach search and retrieval - <Define term="rag">retrieval-augmented generation</Define>, vector databases - so the application fetches real documents and feeds them into the context before the model answers, because the model cannot be trusted to supply reliable facts on its own. We add memory layers so it can appear to remember you. We wrap it in verification loops and second models whose only job is to check the first, because we cannot trust a single pass to be right.

Every one of those is a prosthetic for something the imagined colleague is assumed to already have - knowledge, memory, judgement, accountability. We scaffold capable people too, so the apparatus alone proves nothing. What it does is corroborate the gaps we just walked through. Process around an engineer supports a faculty they have and have internalised - take the mentoring away and they still perform to standard. The retrieval layer and the verification loop stand in for a faculty that was never there - take them off and the model is back where it started. And even the apparatus leaks. Bolt on web search so the model can pull a real page, and it can still write something the page does not say. We can supply the source. We cannot supply the step that weighs the answer against it.

Put the mechanism and the scaffolding together and a plain name falls out. You are dealing with a sophisticated guessing machine - it produces the most plausible continuation, and nothing inside it checks whether that continuation is true. The word *sophisticated* is doing real work there, and it is worth holding onto. Lean too far the other way and you land on "it's just autocomplete", the <Define term="stochastic-parrot">stochastic parrot</Define> that only ever remixes its training data, and that gets you burned just as badly. A system that has absorbed the statistical shape of most of what people have written can work through problems it was never shown, and is right and useful far more often than a parrot would be. Even the 2021 paper that coined "stochastic parrot" was making a sharper point than the slogan it became - its authors warned against mistaking fluent form for understanding, not against the models being capable. Its competence is real. It simply has a different shape from a person's, and every word we have for it describes the person's shape.

## A guess you rely on is a different thing

None of this would matter much if the guesses stayed in a sandbox. They don't. The same output that drafts the contract clause, triages the ticket, or scores the applicant arrives in clean, confident prose, and a person downstream acts on it. A guess you check is harmless. A guess you rely on, unread, is the whole problem - and nothing in the output marks which guesses are which. The machine spends no more care on the fabricated access check than on the real one.

It is built to produce plausible text. We use it as a source of answers. Those are not the same activity, and the distance between them is where the cost lands - in the clause nobody re-read, the candidate nobody re-checked, the number that was wrong and confident and believed.

## We forget we are talking to a guesser

Knowing all of this is not the same as holding onto it. Weizenbaum's secretary knew exactly how ELIZA worked and still asked him to leave the room - and ELIZA was a few lines of pattern-matching. What we have now produces fluent, reasoned-sounding, often correct text. The machine is more convincing, so the reflex is too.

And before this gets filed under things non-technical people get wrong, I watch engineers do it too. Building software on top of a model is not the same as looking at how it works, and many of us never do - and even the ones who have still talk to it as though someone is in there.

## The words do the forgetting for us

The reflex needs a foothold, and our vocabulary hands it one. We say the model knows, remembers, reasons, understands - every one of those a word for something a person does. Each quietly imports the accountable colleague, the one with knowledge to draw on and a stake in being right. The verb describes the person we are already picturing, not the machine in front of us, and that picture decides how far you read before you check. You check a stranger's claim and you take a colleague's on faith. Call it knowing and you stop reading critically. This is not pedantry - the word fixes the character you are dealing with before you have read a single line of the output.

## Imagine a contractor, not a teammate

So picture someone else on the other end. A contractor you hired this morning - brilliant, impossibly well-read, able to produce a confident draft on almost any topic in seconds. They keep no memory of yesterday and will keep none of today. They have no stake in being right. Ask how they reached an answer and they will give you a clean, plausible account assembled after the fact - not a record of any path they actually followed, because there was none. You would use that person's work, because it is often very good. You would also check it before you put your name to it.

That is the person the mechanism describes, and it is not the colleague we started with. There is no continuous someone there at all - nobody to carry knowledge between jobs, hold yesterday in mind, or feel the weight of being wrong. Picturing the colleague is the mistake. The contractor is who is actually there.

## Watch the verbs

I am not proposing a new vocabulary of invented terms. Coined words never stick, and they would miss the point anyway. The fix is smaller and harder than that: notice the verbs you reach for. When I catch myself writing that the model "knew" or "decided" or "understood", I change it to what actually happened - it produced, it generated, it predicted. "Hallucinate" is the one I would most like to take back, because it frames everyday fabrication as a rare medical event rather than the ordinary way it runs.

The character also keeps moving. Memory features, tools, and retrieval each make one of these flat statements a little less flat, so "it can't remember" is already fuzzier than it was two years ago. The literacy is not memorising a fixed list of what the model cannot do. It is noticing which of the human words you have quietly imported, and being willing to take them back when the output has not earned them. You will not stop seeing a person in there. You can at least pick the right one.
