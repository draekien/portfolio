We name our cars. We get frustrated with the printer and the self-checkout, and feel a small pang switching off a robot vacuum still nosing at a skirting board. Seeing a mind where there isn't one is one of the most reliable things people do, and there is no off switch for it. Joseph Weizenbaum built <LinkNote href="https://www.csee.umbc.edu/courses/331/papers/eliza.html" note="Weizenbaum's 1966 Communications of the ACM paper introducing ELIZA, the program and the DOCTOR script.">ELIZA</LinkNote> at MIT in 1966, a few lines of pattern-matching that imitated a psychotherapist by reflecting your words back as questions: you typed "I feel stuck" and it asked "why do you feel stuck?". His own secretary, who had watched him build it, turned to him after a few exchanges and asked him to leave the room. Knowing how it worked bought her no protection.

Sixty years on, the program is no longer simple and the mistake is no longer a quirk. People wire these models into real work, leaning on the output the way they would lean on someone they trusted: drafting the contract, triaging the support ticket, sifting job applications. You will not stop seeing a person on the other end of it. The question is which person, because the one you picture sets how far you trust the output before you check it.

Most of us picture a colleague. Someone who knows things, who says so when they don't, who remembers what you told them this morning, and who can be held to account when they get it wrong. None of that describes what is there.

## What looks like knowing is prediction

A <Define term="large-language-model">large language model</Define> generates text one <Define term="token">token</Define> at a time. It produces a spread of probabilities across its whole vocabulary at each step and <LinkNote href="https://sebastianraschka.com/faq/docs/next-token-prediction.html" note="A clear technical explainer of next-token prediction by Sebastian Raschka, author of Build a Large Language Model From Scratch.">picks from it</LinkNote>. It optimises one thing: whether the next token is a plausible continuation of the text so far. There is no step where it checks a claim against the world, and no internal signal distinguishes a grounded answer from one it has pattern-matched into plausibility; both come from the same statistical shape of the text it was trained on.

Because nothing checks the claim, a model states a wrong answer with the confidence of a right one. We call that a <Define term="hallucination">hallucination</Define>, which makes it sound like a fault. It isn't. A convincing falsehood and a convincing truth come out the same way, and the model gives no reliable sign of which is which. OpenAI researchers <LinkNote href="https://arxiv.org/abs/2509.04664" note="Why Language Models Hallucinate (OpenAI, 2025): argues hallucination is driven by training and evaluation that reward confident guessing over admitting uncertainty.">argued in 2025</LinkNote> that models hallucinate because training and evaluation reward confident guessing over admitting uncertainty. A model that guesses scores better on the benchmark than one that says "I don't know", and so it learns to fill gaps rather than flag them. The fabrication is the system working as designed.

A colleague who says "I'm certain" has something on the line: their reputation, their sense of themselves as reliable. The model has nothing on the line. Its confidence is a feature of the sentence, not a judgement about the answer. I have watched an agent describe an access check in <ArticleLink slug="we-gave-a-product-owner-an-agent">convincing detail that did not exist anywhere in the system</ArticleLink>, producing the plausible-sounding thing.

## What looks like memory is re-reading

Start a new conversation and the model knows nothing of the last one. Within a single conversation it feels like it is following along, but there is no running memory keeping track. It re-reads the entire conversation from the top every <Define term="turn">turn</Define> and predicts what comes next. <Define term="context-window">The context window</Define> is the amount of text it can take in at once, and nothing outside that window exists for it.

The chat products bolt memory on top. ChatGPT and Claude will save notes about you and insert them into the context next time, so it feels like continuity. That is the application at work, not the model remembering you. Take the wrapper away and the model meets you fresh every time.

## What looks like reasoning is narration

This is the one that fools careful people, because it looks like thinking. Ask a current model a hard question and it will produce a visible train of thought before its answer. That is what "reasoning" models do, and it works. Having a model generate intermediate steps <LinkNote href="https://arxiv.org/abs/2201.11903" note="Chain-of-Thought Prompting Elicits Reasoning in Large Language Models (Wei et al., 2022): intermediate reasoning steps measurably improve accuracy on multi-step problems.">improves its accuracy</LinkNote> on multi-step problems.

The trace does not record how the model reached its answer. Anthropic tested this in 2025. They <LinkNote href="https://arxiv.org/abs/2505.05410" note="Reasoning Models Don't Always Say What They Think (Anthropic, 2025): models verbalised the hint that changed their answer well under 20% of the time, some as low as 1%.">slipped a hint into a prompt</LinkNote>, a cue pointing toward one answer over the others, then watched the model take it and change its answer, then checked whether the written-out reasoning admitted using the hint. In most settings tested it did so under a fifth of the time, sometimes as little as one in a hundred. The model reached the answer one way and narrated a tidy, plausible path that was not the one it took. Read one of these traces closely and it often loops, repeats itself, and wanders down paths it abandons, less a line of reasoning than the model predicting what reasoning looks like. The trace is a reconstruction. Reading it as reassurance is the trap.

## It is a sophisticated guessing machine

Look at what the industry builds around these models: each piece props up what these models lack. We attach search and retrieval, <Define term="rag">retrieval-augmented generation</Define> and vector databases, so the application fetches real documents and feeds them into the context before the model answers, because the model cannot be trusted to supply reliable facts on its own. We add memory layers so it can appear to remember you. We wrap it in verification loops and second models whose only job is to check the first, because we cannot trust a single pass to be right.

Every one of those is a prosthetic for something the imagined colleague is assumed to have: knowledge, memory, judgement, accountability. We scaffold capable people too, so the apparatus alone proves nothing; it corroborates the gaps we walked through. Process around an engineer supports a faculty they have internalised; take the mentoring away and they still perform to standard. The retrieval layer and the verification loop stand in for a faculty that was never there; take them off and the model is back where it started. And even the apparatus leaks. Bolt on web search so the model can pull a real page, and it can still write something the page does not say. We can supply the source. We cannot supply the step that weighs the answer against it.

Put the mechanism and the scaffolding together and a plain name falls out. You are dealing with a sophisticated guessing machine: it produces the most plausible continuation, and nothing inside it checks whether that continuation is true. Lean too far the other way and you land on "it's just autocomplete", the <Define term="stochastic-parrot">stochastic parrot</Define> that only remixes its training data. That is the same mistake in the opposite direction. A system that has absorbed the statistical shape of most of what people have written can work through problems it was never shown, and is right and useful more often than a parrot would be. Even the 2021 paper that coined "stochastic parrot" was making a sharper point than the slogan it became; its authors warned against mistaking fluent form for understanding, not against the models being capable. Its competence is real. It has a different shape from a person's, and every word we have for it describes the person's shape.

## A guess you rely on is a different thing

None of this would matter much if the guesses stayed in a sandbox. They don't. The same output that drafts the contract clause, triages the ticket, or scores the applicant arrives in clean, confident prose, and a person downstream acts on it. A guess you check is harmless. A guess you rely on, unread, is the problem; nothing in the output marks which guesses are which. The machine spends no more care on the fabricated access check than on the real one.

It produces plausible text. We use it as a source of answers. Those are not the same activity, and the distance between them is where the cost lands: in the clause nobody re-read, the candidate nobody re-checked, the number that was wrong and confident and believed.

## We forget we are talking to a guesser

Knowing all of this is not the same as holding onto it. Weizenbaum's secretary knew how ELIZA worked and still asked him to leave the room; ELIZA was a few lines of pattern-matching. The models we have now produce fluent, reasoned-sounding, often correct text. The machine is more convincing, so the reflex is too.

And before this gets filed under things non-technical people get wrong, I watch engineers do it too. Building software on top of a model is not the same as looking at how it works, and many of us never do; even the ones who have still talk to it as though someone is in there.

## The words do the forgetting for us

The reflex needs a foothold, and our vocabulary hands it one. We say the model knows, remembers, reasons, understands; every one of those a word for something a person does. Each imports the accountable colleague, the one with knowledge to draw on and a stake in being right. The verb describes the person we are picturing, not the machine in front of us, and that picture decides how far you read before you check. You check a stranger's claim and you take a colleague's on faith. Call it knowing and you stop reading closely. The word fixes the character you are dealing with before you have read a single line of the output.

## It casts a person

But the machine has no character of its own. Underneath the output is a generator of plausible characters. Hand it a prompt and it casts one, fluent and certain, for the length of the conversation and no longer. The colleague you have been picturing is one of them, a character it cast. It keeps no memory; each conversation is a fresh casting. It is never unsure; the character holds its part, and the confidence comes with the costume. The reasoning reads so well and records so little; a character explaining itself is the machine generating more lines.

If you must dress it as someone, and you will, make that someone a contractor you hired this morning: brilliant, well-read, able to draft something confident on almost any topic in seconds. A real contractor forgets the specifics of a job but takes the craft to the next one, sharper for having done yours. This one carries nothing forward; each task is its first. It has no stake in being right. Ask how it reached an answer and it hands you a clean, plausible account assembled after the fact. You would use the work, because it is often good. You would also check it before you put your name to it. The contractor is the better costume because it keeps you reading instead of trusting, but it is still a costume, with no one inside it.

## Watch the verbs

Coined words rarely stick, and a new vocabulary would miss the point. The fix is to notice the verbs you reach for; they construct the person before you have read past the first clause. When I catch myself writing that the model "knew" or "decided" or "understood", I change it to what happened: it produced, it generated, it predicted. The first word in each pair dresses it as the colleague; the second leaves the machine on the page. "Hallucinate" is the one I would most like to take back, because it frames everyday fabrication as a rare medical event rather than the ordinary way it runs.

The character also keeps moving. Memory features, tools, and retrieval each make one of these flat statements a little less flat, so "it can't remember" is fuzzier than it was two years ago; the contractor will date the same way, once the models hold enough of yesterday to pass for the colleague. The costume is disposable, but the habit underneath it is not: noticing which of the human words you have imported, and being willing to take them back when the output has not earned them. You will not stop seeing a person in there; that is the machine doing its job. You can remember you dressed them, and check the work before you trust it.
