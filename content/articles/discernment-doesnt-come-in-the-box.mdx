A senior engineer on my team shipped a React feature. They are genuinely excellent: years of deep backend work in .NET, the kind of developer whose code I rarely have to question. They had barely written any frontend. So they reached for AI and let it generate the React.

The <Define term="pull-request">pull request</Define> looked finished. It was not. The code used an <Define term="use-effect" code>effect</Define> to keep a copy of *derived state*: values you can compute from data you already hold, kept instead as a second copy that quietly drifts out of sync. Heavy computations re-ran on every keystroke with no <Define term="memoisation">memoisation</Define>. And it was a few giant components nursing tangles of internal state, where small focused pieces would have been clearer.

None of this is exotic. React's own documentation has a page titled <LinkNote href="https://react.dev/learn/you-might-not-need-an-effect" note="React's official guide to when an effect is unnecessary: it shows how to calculate values during render, memoise expensive work, and move logic into event handlers instead.">"You Might Not Need an Effect"</LinkNote> about the first two mistakes, and my team has written rules for each. I left review comments explaining every one. They took them back to the AI and tried again; the result was no better, because they could not tell whether the model's next attempt had fixed the problem or just relocated it.

They lacked one thing, and it was neither effort nor seniority: the ability to look at React and know good from bad. In the end I rewrote the feature from scratch.

This incident is a small version of a much larger bet companies are making. The pressure everywhere is to point AI at output metrics, and hitting those numbers means automating the work that grows engineers. The damage never reaches this quarter's dashboard; it surfaces years later, in the seniors you no longer have.

## Discernment doesn't transfer between domains

It would be easy to read that as "a senior got sloppy." That misses the point. They were operating outside their domain, and the judgment that makes them excellent in .NET does not carry across the fence.

The useful fault line runs between *in-domain and out-of-domain* work, not junior versus senior - and the AI conversation keeps missing it. An expert frontend developer would have generated the same bad React and caught it on the first read; a backend expert cannot, because the catching is the expertise, and expertise is local. It is built one domain at a time and does not transfer because you are good somewhere else.

AI changes what each side can do, but not symmetrically. It raises the *floor*: anyone can produce code that compiles and roughly runs in a language they never learned. What it does not raise on its own is the *ceiling*, the judgment of whether that code is actually good, because that has to be earned rather than generated.

## AI raises the floor and hides the ceiling

That gap is dangerous, not just disappointing. AI lets people produce confident, plausible output well beyond their competence, and plausible is exactly the failure mode that gets through: obvious garbage is thrown away, while code that only looks right reaches review.

So the cost moves. It lands on whoever *can* judge the output: the seniors. Every out-of-domain AI submission that fails the sniff test is now a senior's problem to catch, explain, and often rewrite. AI was supposed to reduce the load on experienced engineers; in my experience it has done the opposite, manufacturing more code that needs an expert eye and giving the author no way to pre-filter it.

The research points the same way. A 2025 study from Microsoft Research and Carnegie Mellon, presented at the CHI human-computer interaction conference, <LinkNote href="https://dl.acm.org/doi/full/10.1145/3706598.3713778" note="A CHI 2025 paper from Microsoft Research and Carnegie Mellon: a survey of knowledge workers finding that the more they trusted AI, the less critical thinking they applied to its output.">surveyed knowledge workers</LinkNote> about their AI use.

Two findings matter. The more someone trusted the AI, the less critical thinking they applied to what it returned. And people leaned hardest on it, with the least scrutiny, exactly when they lacked the expertise to evaluate its output. This is a broad pattern across those surveyed, not a quirk of a few - though it rests on what people reported about their own habits, not on measured behaviour.

## The work we automate is the work that makes seniors

Organisations deploy AI toward throughput: lines of code, pull requests, release frequency. Pick a number that looks like productivity, push AI at it, and AI will hit it. The moment you optimise a measure this hard it stops meaning anything - <Define term="goodharts-law">Goodhart's Law</Define> with an engine bolted on.

But that framing hides a slower, worse cost almost nobody is pricing in. The work we are most eager to automate, the boilerplate, the first stumbling implementation, the bug you stare at for an hour, is the *exact* work that turns a junior into a senior. Discernment is accumulated rather than taught, built through reps: writing the wrong thing, feeling why it is wrong, getting feedback, trying again. That loop is the only path from floor to ceiling I have watched actually work, and it does not come in the box.

The obvious objection is that we have heard this before: compilers would rot the discipline of managing memory, Stack Overflow and the frameworks would raise a generation who could not reason from first principles. Each time the panic faded, because judgment relocated one level up the stack. What makes AI different is what sits under the abstraction. A compiler is deterministic and trustworthy, so you can stop checking its output and build on top of it; AI is confidently wrong at random, so the layer never goes solid enough to stand on. And what it automates is not the domain but the reps inside it: the junior writing React with AI never writes the wrong React and feels why, so the in-domain practice that would have built their discernment never happens.

Remember my senior's PR. I told them precisely what was wrong, and it did not help, because being told is not the same as having learned. I have watched this with juniors for years: a senior points out the mistake, the junior nods, and three weeks later makes it again, because the correction was delivered rather than earned. That struggle is the learning process itself, not friction in its way, and it is exactly what we are handing to the machine.

So an organisation optimising for output with AI does more than game a metric: it dismantles its own pipeline. Today's seniors did, by hand, the work AI now does. Automate that work away for the next cohort and they never make the climb, and a few years later the people who could have reviewed the AI's output are the ones you forgot to grow.

## Cognitive debt comes due later

At the individual level the cost is sharper. Researchers at the MIT Media Lab call it <Define term="cognitive-debt">cognitive debt</Define>, in <LinkNote href="https://www.media.mit.edu/projects/your-brain-on-chatgpt/" note="A 2025 MIT Media Lab study (a preprint) that measured brain activity as people wrote essays with and without an LLM, reporting weaker neural engagement and recall among heavy AI users.">a 2025 study</LinkNote> modelled on technical debt, the shortcut that costs more the longer you defer it: leaning on an LLM saves effort now and charges interest later, in weaker memory, less original thinking, and a shallower grasp of work you only nominally produced.

People who wrote essays with an LLM showed the weakest brain engagement and the lowest sense of ownership, and 83% could not quote a single sentence from what they had *just* written. As the lead author, Nataliya Kosmyna, put it: "There is no cognitive credit card. You cannot pay this debt off."

Honesty about the evidence matters here, since judging sources is the whole subject. That result is a preprint, with 54 participants and only 18 in the crucial session that switched people between the LLM and writing unaided, so I would not lean on it alone. But it matches what I see on real teams, and names something you will recognise if you have ever shipped with AI and then could not maintain the result without it: you built it but did not learn it, so when the tool is gone, so is your ability to keep it alive.

## Point the tool at the engineer, not the number

None of this is an argument against AI, only about where you aim it. The same tool that hollows out a junior when pointed at an output metric can accelerate that junior's growth when pointed at the junior instead.

I have made this argument before in a different shape. I once <ArticleLink slug="skills-that-teach">spent a month rewriting a skill</ArticleLink> and concluded that a good instruction *teaches* rather than dictates: it transfers judgment instead of just listing steps. This is that idea one level up. Point AI at the engineer and its job is to transfer judgment, not to spare them the thinking that builds it.

In practice, that has meant a few habits. Use AI as a rubber duck: explaining the problem to it surfaces the answer. Use it to attack your own design, not defend it: ask for the strongest case against the approach you have fallen in love with. Ask it *why*, relentlessly, rather than *what*, so the reasoning lands with you and not just the code. And use it to generate learning material: before someone works in an unfamiliar domain, have AI build them a graded set of exercises against your own codebase, instead of doing that domain's work for them.

There is a lever here for whoever owns the metrics, too, and it falls out of the opening story. That PR looked finished and would have sailed through any throughput count while quietly generating a pile of rework. So measure the rework: review-rejection rate, churn on recently shipped code, how often a change has to be redone. These catch the cost that lines-per-day cannot, and unlike "this junior's judgment improved" they are numbers you can chart.

## What I don't have figured out

The honest tradeoffs are real, and they explain why organisations reach for the output metric anyway. Automating for throughput is sometimes simply correct: plenty of boilerplate teaches nobody anything, a throwaway prototype deserves no ceremony, a real deadline can outweigh a learning opportunity. The rule I use is to automate freely when the code is disposable or the domain is already in the team's wheelhouse, and to protect the loop when someone is building durable judgment in a domain they will own.

Growth-oriented use also costs more in the dimensions a metrics culture punishes. It is slower, it spends scarce senior time, and "this junior's judgment improved" has no number you can chart per day. The output metric wins by default because it is *legible*, whatever it actually measures.

Two counterarguments deserve an answer. A firm could buy seniors instead of growing them, letting AI cover the floor while it hires experience away; but that works for one company and fails for all at once, draining a senior pool already tightening for this reason. And the next generation may build a different discernment, steering AI rather than reading raw code, though that skill is still earned through reps and feedback, which an output-obsessed culture strips out either way. I should be honest that this is a forecast: no one has yet watched an AI-raised cohort fail to produce seniors, because not enough time has passed. I reason from a mechanism and from my own teams, not a settled result.

There is even a failure mode in my own prescription: "use AI to grow your people" curdles into a hollow metric the moment someone counts hours-mentored-by-AI. The goal is discernment in the engineer, and you cannot escape the work of judging whether it is actually growing.

So I will end where I am, without the answer. I am convinced that pointing AI at output metrics mortgages the next generation of engineers to flatter this quarter's dashboard, and equally convinced the same tool could speed the climb from junior to senior faster than anything before it. What I do not have is the pipeline that reliably does the second thing. That is the work worth doing, and I would rather more of us were building it than racing to the bottom of a metric.
