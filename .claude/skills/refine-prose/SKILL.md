# Refine Prose

A draft that is true and well-shaped can still read badly, sentence by sentence and at the seams where one paragraph hands off to the next. This pass works at that level. It does not argue with what the piece says or how it is built - the [article-writing](../article-writing/SKILL.md) skill owns substance and structure - it works on how the prose reads, line by line and across those seams, against the house voice the piece is already trying to hold. Unlike a review, it ends with the edits made, not a list handed back.

The method is one focused reader per editorial concern, run independently, with an editor synthesising their findings and applying only the changes that survive judgment. A single read tightens a few obvious lines and tires before the rest. Splitting the read by concern lets each pass hunt one fault to exhaustion, and a passage two concerns flag independently is almost always the one most worth reworking.

## Name the concerns for this pass

A refinement pass targets a named set of faults, not "make it better". The author usually states them, and when they do, those are the concerns verbatim. When they don't, draw from the faults that recur in this voice:

- **Claim-then-refute** - sentence A asserts something, sentence B takes it back. The "it's A, not B" and "not A, it's B" shapes are the compact form; the same move stretched across a paragraph is a set-up sentence that exists only to be knocked down. Let the fact stand on its own.
- **Claim-colon** - "claim: explanation" used as a sentence's default shape, where a period or a connector would carry it. Reserve the colon for a true list, a quotation, or a deliberate label-opener.
- **Restatement and circling** - a point already made, said again later in fresh words, or prose that loops back to ground already covered. It reads as progress but adds nothing. Trust the reader to hold the earlier fact, and keep the momentum forward.
- **Compounds that crowd ideas** - two or more distinct ideas welded into one sentence with "and", a semicolon, or a dash, where each would carry more weight standing alone.
- **Metaphor that explains** - a metaphor doing the explanatory work a plain sentence already did. If the concept is clear without it, the metaphor is decoration; reserve it for emphasis, not explanation.
- **Performed prose** - sentences reaching to sound clever: the word-of-the-day where a plain word is exact, the aphorism, the ornament that does not earn its place.
- **Self-narration** - the writing describing its own moves instead of just making them.
- **Awkward progressive** - a continuous "-ing" verb standing where the simple present or an infinitive reads cleaner. "the question nobody is asking", "the work done training juniors" carry an ongoing-action nuance the sentence does not want; "nobody asks", "done to train juniors" land flatter and more direct. Keep the progressive only where the action being ongoing is the point.
- **Undercutting tail** - a clause tacked onto the end that takes back the stake the sentence just set, typically a second, milder consequence ("...and someone clears it up eventually", "...but it gets fixed anyway") that drains the urgency of the first. Two opposed outcomes in one sentence leave the reader no clear stake. End on the strongest consequence and let it stand.
- **Stranded locational frame** - a spatial or directional word (upstream and downstream, above and below, across and up) placed far from the thing it positions, so the reader has to reconstruct what sits where. Keep the frame next to its antecedent; when the word that orients an idea drifts from the idea, the orientation is lost. This applies across a passage too - a heading that locates the section ("the cost lands downstream") strands its reader if the body then places the weight elsewhere.
- **Paragraph cohesion** - the seam between paragraphs rather than the line within one. A transition that jumps to the next topic with no hand-off; a "this", "that", or "that much" whose referent sits a paragraph or more back, so the reader climbs to recover it; a hook or promise raised in one paragraph whose payoff the following ones defer past the point it was set up for. Each paragraph should connect to the one before, and a point should pay off near where it is raised. Argument structure stays with article-writing - this is the connective tissue, not the skeleton.

These map onto the project's established house voice and prose conventions - the same standards the [article-writing](../article-writing/SKILL.md) skill and the prose-style skill hold the draft to. Brief every reader with that guidance so its findings target the established voice, not generic style advice the author has already rejected.

## Run one reader per concern, in isolation

Give each concern its own reader, each working the full piece with no knowledge of the others. Independence is the point: when two readers land on the same sentence without coordinating, the convergence is signal, and pooling them early collapses them toward one averaged voice that misses the sharp single-concern catches.

Each reader returns findings only - it does not edit. For every finding: the sentence quoted verbatim, one line on which concern it violates and why, and a proposed replacement. A bare "this is wordy" is not a finding.

Hold each reader to selectivity, not coverage. A surface pattern is not a fault by itself, and a reader that flags every instance of one buries the few that matter. The legitimate uses it must leave alone:

- A colon doing real work. The tic is the colon used as a default sentence shape, not a genuine list or quotation.
- A run of events about one subject. Serial happenings to one actor are a single idea, not a crowded compound.
- A precise, common technical term. Refinement does not swap a correct word for a simpler, vaguer one.
- A metaphor carrying genuine emphasis. The fault is the metaphor that re-explains something already clear, not the one that lands a point.
- A progressive carrying genuine ongoing aspect. The fault is the "-ing" standing in for a plain present, not the one describing an action that is actually continuous.
- A clean break between sections. A new section opening a fresh line is not a broken seam; the fault is a missing hand-off where the prose means to continue, not a deliberate reset.

## Edit with judgment, not by transcription

The readers produce findings; the editor decides which to apply. Read the whole piece fresh before changing a line - findings arrive sentence by sentence, and only a full read shows which changes serve the argument and which quietly cost it something.

Weight the findings:

- A sentence multiple readers flagged is high-confidence. Rework it.
- When concerns collide on one passage, the fix is a single reworking that satisfies all of them, not each proposed replacement applied in turn. Applying one reader's replacement and then the next reader's on top often manufactures the very fault a third reader was hunting.

Decline a change when it costs more than it saves:

- A closing callback survives only if it adds a new judgment. A callback that merely restates an earlier line goes; one that turns the earlier line into a verdict stays.
- A concrete line doing real work outranks the pattern it appears to violate. Cut ornament, not evidence.
- A deliberate cadence the author chose is not automatically a fault. Flatten an aphorism to its plain facts only when the cadence is performing, and keep the facts either way.

For a restatement, the decision is delete or relocate. If the later mention adds nothing, cut it. If it says the thing better than the first mention did, move that strength back to where the point is first made and rework the two into one.

Apply selectively. The measure of the pass is targeted improvement, not the number of edits.

## Verify the survivors

After editing, search the text again for each concern's signature - the connectors, the colons, the repeated phrasings. For every match still standing, say why it stays. A clean pass is one where every survivor is justified, not one where the count dropped. Paragraph cohesion has no search signature - re-read the seams between paragraphs instead, checking each demonstrative resolves to something close behind it. Then confirm the piece still builds, so no edit broke a component, a link, or the frontmatter.

## Gotchas

- **Mechanical application reintroduces the fault.** Applying each reader's replacement verbatim, in sequence, is how a claim-refute fix becomes a compound and a compound fix becomes a restatement. The synthesis is the work; the findings are raw material.
- **Counting is not verifying.** A search that returns zero is meaningless if the legitimate uses were collateral. Inspect the survivors and the casualties, not the tally.
- **Churn masquerading as refinement.** Rewriting lines that were already fine inflates the diff and risks the voice. If a sentence reads clean against every concern, leave it.
- **Generic advice from a reader.** A finding the author could get from any style guide wastes the pass. Brief every reader with the house voice so each catch is specific to this piece.
